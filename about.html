About

Glasses on your
screen, not your face.

DOA started as a simple frustration and turned into a two-year technical journey through three platforms, two dead ends, and one insight that changed everything.

Where it started

On November 4th, 2024, the idea was simple: if your glasses correct your vision by bending light before it reaches your eye, why couldn't a screen do the same thing — by adjusting the image before displaying it?

The answer turned out to be: it can. But getting there took longer than expected.

The journey

Three platforms. Two hard stops. One product that finally works.

Nov 2024

The idea

The concept clicks: pre-distort the screen image so that after passing through the user's imperfect optics, it arrives at the retina in focus. Like noise-cancelling headphones, but for vision.

Dec 2024

Android app — first attempt

Development starts on a mobile app. The vision correction works within the app itself — PDFs, videos, the built-in browser. It shows real promise.

Aug 2025
Hard stop

Mobile hits a wall

Both Android and iOS security models block apps from applying overlays system-wide. Correction is locked to the app itself — useless for everyday screen use. Development pauses.

Sep 2025

Browser extension — second attempt

A browser extension could apply correction to anything viewed in the browser. Development begins. Progress looks good.

Late 2025
Hard stop

Browser hits a wall

Browser security models block extensions from reading pixel data across tabs and pages at the level required for real-time correction. Another dead end — by design, not by bug.

Now
In progress

Desktop app — the right path

Windows Graphics Capture gives OS-level screen access. The correction engine processes every frame in real time. The correction works across every app, window, and content type. This is the one.

The science

DOA isn't magic and it isn't new. The underlying concept was demonstrated by MIT Media Lab researchers in 2014 — they called it "putting the glasses on the display rather than on your head." Their prototype used a pinhole mask over a screen to project slightly different images to different parts of the pupil, simulating a focused image for users with refractive errors.

DOA takes the same principle and implements it entirely in software — a correction engine that runs at 60fps and applies a calibrated layer to your screen based on your exact prescription in real time.

The noise-cancellation analogy

Active noise-cancelling headphones work by measuring the sound coming in and generating an anti-sound — the exact inverse waveform — so the two cancel each other out and you hear silence.

DOA works the same way for vision. It measures how your eye distorts incoming light (your prescription) and applies the inverse distortion to the screen image. By the time the light from the screen reaches your retina, the distortions cancel out — and you see clearly.

No hardware. No lenses. Just math applied to pixels before they're displayed.

The correction isn't perfect — it's an approximation based on your stated prescription and screen DPI. It works best for everyday reading and productivity use. It doesn't replace glasses for all activities. But for the hours most people spend in front of a screen, it's genuinely useful.

Where this is going

The desktop app is the beginning, not the destination. The real goal is to make this technology invisible — built into the displays and operating systems people already use, so nobody has to install anything.

🖥️

Better correction over time

Feedback from real users will improve how DOA calibrates correction parameters to different prescription profiles — moving from static math toward personalized accuracy.

🍎

macOS

A macOS port using ScreenCaptureKit and Metal. Same concept, different platform. After Windows is stable.

🔬

Efficacy study + provisional patent

Independent validation of the correction quality. A prerequisite for the conversations that matter most.

📺

Monitor OEM integration

The endgame — working with display manufacturers to build the correction directly into firmware. A monitor that corrects for your eyes without any software install.

📱

Mobile OS integration

Compositor-level integration on Android and iOS — correction applied at the OS level, before any app renders. The version of the original mobile idea that actually works.
